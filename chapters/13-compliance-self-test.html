M Calibration CORE
QUESTION 1 OF 4
“GDPR and the EU Data Act both create rights around data. What’s the key difference?”
MODEL ANSWER

GDPR covers personal data — data about identified or identifiable individuals. Rights: access, erasure, portability.

EU Data Act covers non-personal machine-generated data — IoT devices, industrial sensors, connected cars. Rights: device users can access the data their devices generate and share it with third parties.

The overlap: if IoT data identifies a person (location data, health sensors), both regulations apply simultaneously.

M Calibration CORE
QUESTION 2 OF 4
“Name the three dimensions of data sovereignty.”
MODEL ANSWER
  1. Physical location — where the servers physically sit.
  2. Legal jurisdiction — whose laws apply to the data.
  3. Operational control — who can actually access or command the data (the CLOUD Act problem: US law can compel US-headquartered companies to hand over data stored anywhere in the world).

Key insight: putting servers in Germany doesn’t solve operational control if the cloud provider is US-headquartered.

HOW CONFIDENT?
M Calibration CORE
QUESTION 3 OF 4
“ISO 27001, SOC 2, ISAE 3402 — one sentence each.”
MODEL ANSWER

ISO 27001 — international standard (certifiable) for information security management systems. You can hold a certificate.

SOC 2 — US-origin audit report (not a certificate) on service organization controls for security, availability, confidentiality, and privacy.

ISAE 3402 — the European/international equivalent of SOC 2. Also an audit report on internal controls.

The anchor: ISO 27001 = you have the system (certification). SOC 2 / ISAE 3402 = here’s the auditor’s report on how your controls performed (attestation, not certification).

HOW CONFIDENT?
M Calibration CORE
QUESTION 4 OF 4
“The EU AI Act classifies AI by risk level. What are the four tiers, and what happens at each?”
MODEL ANSWER

Prohibited — banned entirely. Social scoring, manipulative AI, real-time biometric surveillance in public.

High-risk — heavily regulated. Article 10 data governance, documented provenance, bias examination, conformity assessment. Hiring, credit scoring, medical devices.

Limited risk — transparency obligations only. Chatbots must disclose they’re AI. Deepfakes must be labeled.

Minimal risk — no obligations. Video games, spam filters, most recommendation systems.

HOW CONFIDENT?

M a senior consultant CORE
M1 — GDPR ERASURE WORKFLOW
“A German client receives an Article 17 deletion request. The customer has been in their system for 5 years. Walk me through what you’d actually do as a BI consultant.”
MODEL ANSWER
  1. Frame the role: You handle the data architecture side. The DPO makes the legal call on whether an exception applies (Art. 17(3) — legal retention, ongoing disputes, public interest).
  2. Map the data across layers: Staging → Raw Vault (Hub + PII Satellite) → Business Vault (derived calculations) → Information Marts (row-level reports) → Backups.
  3. Execute: DELETE from PII Satellite rows for this hash key + INSERT tombstone record (audit trail without retaining PII). Hub can stay if business key is non-PII; requires Artificial Hub pattern if business key is PII.
  4. Document everything: What was deleted, from where, when, who authorized it, under which request ID.
  5. Third-party notification: If data was shared with third parties, notify them (Art. 17(2)).

Follow-up insight: The hardest part in practice is data lineage — knowing where ALL copies live. Without a data catalog, you can’t find the backups, the marts, the derived tables.

KEY CONCEPTS TO PRACTICE

These are the lines worth rehearsing out loud. Say them, don’t just read them.

ON THE GDPR-DV TENSION

“GDPR was written for transactional systems where you find a row and delete it. Data Vault was built to never delete anything. Resolving that isn’t just a legal problem or a technical problem — it’s both at once. The solution is architectural: PII Satellite isolation, Artificial Hubs, tombstone records. You design for compliance upfront so you never have to break the vault to honor the law.”

ON DATA VAULT’S COMPLIANCE ADVANTAGE

“Data Vault’s design choices — insert-only loading, load timestamps, source tracking on every row — weren’t built for compliance. But they happen to be exactly what regulators ask for under GDPR, DORA, and the AI Act. That’s not coincidence — it’s the same underlying principle: if you can reconstruct what happened and prove where data came from, you can demonstrate compliance.”

ON NOT KNOWING SOMETHING

“I’d want to think through the specifics before answering that definitively — my instinct is [X], but I’d verify [Y] before committing to it in a client conversation.”
